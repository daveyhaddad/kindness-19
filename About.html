About Me

By Aneska Schock

On the first day of quarantine, (when I *was* highly motivated to come up with goals and intentions for my time in isolation) I looked through some of my old journals and writing when I found my reflections from 2019 and goals for 2020. This January, I decided to try to write down and remember all of the days that I was impacted (positively or negatively) in the last 365 days. As I thought through the individual days of 2019 and wrote them down, I quickly realized that 1) I was writing down that almost every single day of the year had impacted me in some way and 2) the days that I didn’t write down, were days when I wasn’t paying attention to my life and cruising on autopilot. By the time I finished this exercise, I began to wonder if it’s possible (to some extent) to choose what you will be impacted by? Some of the days I wrote were days when I was particularly amazed by the way light hit the branches of trees or how the man who was asking for money on the street painted me a picture. These aren’t huge things, but I was impacted by those things because I chose to be and I documented them.


I was completely caught off guard by this realization. Could it really be true that every single day has the potential to be impactful and can change my perspectives on the world? I had never entertained this idea before. There has never been a time in my life when I have woken up for my everyday life and thought “today I can be impacted by my life and I can choose what I want to be impacted by.” Usually when I wake up for my daily life, I lay in bed for longer than I would like to admit thinking about the to-do list for the day and trying to muster up any motivation to get out of my cozy bed.


So, as I considered the future on day 1of quarantine, I wondered how it would be possible to choose what I am impacted by in a world where I am constantly inundated with messages of fear, uncertainty, a crashing economy, and a pandemic that is becoming increasingly scary. I am much more prone to be impacted by those headlines than focus on the good and kind acts that are happening just outside my doorstep, and I am pretty sure that I am not alone in that.


I decided that if we (as a society) were going to make it through this thing and not only be impacted by all the disheartening things going on in our world, it’s important to document all of the hundreds and thousands of small stories of hope and kindness that our communities are offering to each other too. So, I began brainstorming different ways that I could make that happen.


I started to imagine what kind of platform I wanted to create and decided that a living map would be a good way to go.


You know those maps that you might find in a dark corner of a coffee shop that is full of pins that people have placed saying, “This is where I am from and that’s important!”? I love those maps. Whenever I see them, I feel simultaneously small and important, connected and hopeful.


My hope is that I can feel those things when I look at this map.


I’m going to be honest, when I first thought of this idea, I was thinking a lot more about how this kind of project could impact our city, my friends, and people who are struggling in isolation (classic, me thinking that I am going to change the world without needing to be changed myself). But, very quickly (aka day 2 and 3 of quarantine) I realized that while maybe this project will positively impact all of those people, I am the one who really needs it. I’ve wrestled with mental health challenges for more than half my life at this point and it turns out that isolation and the world seeming to crumble to the ground don’t really help with those pre-existing challenges and make it increasingly difficult for me to see all the kindness and goodness happening too.


The truth is, I need this map. And maybe you do too.


So, my hope is that this can be a place where we, together, can fight for each other to see the kindness that is spreading in our world, find creative ways to help, and hold onto the big hope that is all around us.


Also, this project would NOT have happened without Davey Haddad who put the website together, set up the map, and continued to work on it when I didn’t want to. So, huge shoutout to him for supporting this big time and putting it all together!
